♿ Accessibility NYC

About:

The Subway system is the lifeblood of NYC. Yet, over 30 years after the passage of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), mobility is not guaranteed for many New Yorkers. People with disabilities, the elderly, and parents with strollers often have a hard, if not impossible, time riding the Subway. This renders the Subway as useless for many members of our population.
Compared to other subway systems, New York fails in comparison in terms of accessibility. Of our 472 stations, only 25% are accessible, whereas Boston and Chicago have 71% and 69% of their total stations accessible, respectively. According to Transit Center’s Access Denied, an able-bodied person who can use stairs in the city’s 472 stations has a total of 222,312 possible station-to-station trips. A person who relies on the elevator only has 11,990 station-to-station possible trips — and that’s assuming the elevator is even working.
Even among stations with elevators, many stations face constant outages. In 2019, the MTA reported over 14,000 elevator outages among accessible stations. This number isn’t even truly representative as it doesn’t account for multi-day outages.
